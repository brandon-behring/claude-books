# Brame / Vanderbilt — "Writing Good Multiple Choice Test Questions" (2013)

## Author + context

- **Cynthia J. Brame**, Vanderbilt University **Center for Teaching** (CFT), 2013. The single most-cited **university testing-center** MCQ guide on the open web (the ACS credentialing guide and countless other institutions cite it). [[itemwriting-acs-credentialing]] lists it in its bibliography.
- Tiered **T3** (a teaching-center practitioner guide). Its value is **accessibility and framing**: it distills the same canon ([[itemwriting-nbme-guide]], [[itemwriting-haladyna-taxonomy]]) into instructor-friendly language and, crucially, **connects each rule to a cognitive rationale** (working memory, learning outcomes, higher-order thinking).
- NOTE on URL: the original `cft.vanderbilt.edu` path 301-redirects after a 2025 site reorg; the verbatim quotes below were fetched from Brame's authoritative mirror at `sites.google.com/view/cynthia-brame/…`, recorded as the canonical_url.

## Core principle / theory

Brame organizes guidance around three pieces — **stem, correct answer, distractors** — and frames the whole exercise as **aligning the item to a learning outcome and a cognitive level**:

- **Stem:** "should be meaningful by itself and should present a definite problem." A definite problem "allows a focus on the learning outcome." Prefer a **question** stem over a sentence-completion stem (less working-memory load). Negatively-state the stem **only** when the learning outcome demands it, and then emphasize the negative with italics/caps.
- **Distractors:** "**Common student errors provide the best source of distractors.**" "All alternatives should be plausible"; implausible alternatives "don't serve as functional distractors and thus should not be used." Keep alternatives **homogeneous in content**, **parallel in form**, **grammar consistent with the stem**, **similar in length**. Don't use "all of the above" / "none of the above".
- **Higher-order thinking:** a stem "that presents a problem that requires application of course principles, analysis of a problem, or evaluation of alternatives is focused on higher-order thinking" — explicitly tying item design to Bloom's upper levels.

## Empirical backing

- Brame's guide is a synthesis, not primary research; it cites the measurement literature (including the Haladyna line and NBME). Its **distractor-from-misconceptions** advice echoes Haladyna's Guideline 30 ("use typical errors of students"); its **plausibility** rule echoes Guideline 29 (96% scholarly endorsement — see [[itemwriting-haladyna-taxonomy]]).
- The **higher-order-thinking** framing is supported by Bloom/Anderson-Krathwohl ([[theory-blooms-taxonomy]]) and by the "multilogical thinking" construct it cites (Morrison & Free, 2001) — designing items that require chaining more than one fact.

## Application to a certification study guide

- Brame supplies the **best one-line operational definition of a good stem** — "meaningful by itself and presents a definite problem" — which is more memorable for contributors than the longer NBME framing and says the same thing.
- The **"common student errors are the best source of distractors"** line is the most quotable justification for our misconception-mining distractor strategy; cite it directly in our authoring guidance.
- The **higher-order-thinking sentence** is the bridge we want: it names *application / analysis / evaluation* (Bloom verbs) as properties of the **stem**, telling authors exactly how to lift an item above recall.

## Concrete recommendations for claude-books

- **Codify the stem rule as: "a Practice-item stem must be meaningful by itself and pose a definite problem."** Put it verbatim in the contributor guide. (Brame 2013.)
- **Source distractors from common reader misconceptions**, treating "common student errors provide the best source of distractors" as the governing principle — and drop any distractor that isn't plausible. (Brame 2013; converges with Haladyna G29/G30.)
- **Prefer question-form stems over completion-form** for our items — reduces working-memory load, fewer grammatical-cue traps.
- **To write an application/analysis item, build the stem so the reader must apply a principle, analyze a situation, or evaluate alternatives** — not merely recognize a definition. Tag each item with the Bloom level the stem targets.
- **Reserve negatively-stated stems for cases the objective truly requires**, and then visually emphasize the negative.
