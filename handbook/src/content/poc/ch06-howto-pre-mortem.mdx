> **You have**: a decision you are about to make that is hard to reverse — a database choice, a vendor lock-in, a directory restructure, an API deprecation, a framework migration. **You want**: Claude to challenge the decision and surface concrete failure modes *before* you commit, plus a draft ADR you can circulate for review.

## Prerequisites

- A decision you can state in one sentence (if you cannot, that is the first problem to solve).
- A Claude Code session, ideally one with context loaded from the relevant part of your codebase.
- 20 minutes.

## Steps

### 1. State the decision precisely

Open a session and paste:

```
I am about to commit to the following decision:

  <one-sentence decision>

Constraints that informed this choice:
  - <constraint 1>
  - <constraint 2>
  - <constraint 3>

Alternatives I considered and rejected:
  - <alt 1> — rejected because <reason>
  - <alt 2> — rejected because <reason>

Do NOT critique the decision yet. First, restate the decision
back to me in your own words so I can confirm you understood
what I'm actually committing to.
```

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Tip">
  The restatement step catches misunderstandings that would otherwise contaminate every later answer.
</MarginNote>

If Claude's restatement matches what you meant, proceed. If not, refine your one-sentence decision before continuing.

### 2. Ask the pre-mortem question

Paste the pre-mortem prompt:

```
Now imagine it is six months from now. We adopted this
decision and it failed badly enough that we're writing a
post-mortem about what went wrong. Write that post-mortem.

Requirements:
  - Be specific. Name the actual failure mode, not "scaling
    problems" or "team friction."
  - Identify the signals we should have noticed at decision
    time that would have predicted this failure.
  - Identify the question we should have asked at decision
    time that would have changed the outcome.
  - Cover at least 3 distinct failure modes, ranked by your
    estimated likelihood.

Be harsh. The point of this exercise is to find what we're
missing, not to validate what we already decided.
```

The "be harsh" clause is the anti-sycophancy lever. Without it Claude tends to soften ("this could potentially be challenging if..."). With it Claude is more likely to name a real failure mode.

### 3. Pressure-test the top risk

Pick the failure mode Claude rated most likely. Ask:

```
Take failure mode #1 and pressure-test it. Answer:

  1. What is the earliest signal that would tell us this
     failure mode is becoming real?
  2. What is the latest moment we could reverse course before
     the failure is committed? (i.e., what's the point of no
     return?)
  3. What's the cheapest mitigation we could put in place at
     decision time that would either prevent this failure or
     make it cheaply reversible?
```

<MarginNote variant="note" label="Note">
  Steps 2 and 3 turn an abstract risk into a concrete check you can write into the ADR.
</MarginNote>

If the cheapest mitigation is genuinely cheap (a feature flag, a one-page abstraction, a configurable timeout), build it in. If the mitigation is itself expensive, that is information about how irreversible the decision really is.

### 4. Repeat for the second risk

Run step 3 again on failure mode #2. Two is usually enough; three starts hitting diminishing returns. If Claude's failure modes #1 and #2 share a root cause, that root cause is the real risk and your decision frame may need to widen.

### 5. Draft the ADR

Ask Claude to produce a draft:

```
Based on this conversation, draft an Architecture Decision
Record with three sections:

  ## Context
  What was true at decision time, what alternatives existed,
  what constraints applied. Keep to ~5 bullets.

  ## Decision
  What we chose and the 1-2 reasons it won over the
  alternatives. Keep to ~3 sentences.

  ## Consequences
  What becomes easier, what becomes harder, and the specific
  signals from the pre-mortem that would tell us we chose
  wrong. Include the cheapest mitigations we agreed to put in
  place. Keep to ~7 bullets total.

Output as Markdown.
```

### 6. Edit the ADR yourself

Read the draft. Two things to check:

- **Are the Consequences specific enough to act on?** "Monitor database performance" is not actionable. "If query p99 exceeds 200ms, revisit the choice of Postgres for the events table" is.
- **Did Claude soften any of the risks?** If the pre-mortem said "the schema migration will block deploys for 30+ minutes" and the ADR says "migrations may take some time," restore the sharper language.

Save the file as `docs/adr/NNNN-<short-slug>.md` (where `NNNN` is the next sequential number in your ADR directory).

### 7. Circulate before committing

Send the ADR to one human reviewer who was not in the Claude session. Ask: "is the Consequences section concrete enough that you'd know how to falsify the decision in six months?" If yes, commit. If no, return to step 3 with the missing concreteness.

## Expected outcome

You leave the session with:

- A list of 3+ named, ranked failure modes for your decision
- For the top 1-2 risks: the earliest detection signal, the point of no return, and the cheapest mitigation
- A draft ADR with specific, falsifiable Consequences
- A reviewer's sign-off that the ADR is actionable

<KeyIdea>
The pre-mortem is not a vote on whether to proceed. It is a way to add **falsifiable trip-wires** to a decision you have already mostly made, so that the failure (if it happens) is caught early and the decision (if it works) is auditable.
</KeyIdea>

## When this fails

- **Claude produces generic risks** — "this might not scale," "the team might struggle." Almost always means step 1 was too vague. Tighten the one-sentence decision and rerun the pre-mortem.
- **The ADR Consequences are all positive** — the pre-mortem produced specific failure modes but the ADR draft glossed them. Rerun step 5 with the explicit instruction "include the three failure modes by name in the Consequences section, with the signals that would detect each."
- **You start arguing with Claude's failure modes** — that is the wrong direction. The failure modes are a free option: if they are real, you have new information; if they are wrong, you can explain *why* in the ADR's Consequences as resolved-risk notes. Either way the ADR is richer.
